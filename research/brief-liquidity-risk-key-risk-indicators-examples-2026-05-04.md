### Common sections across competitors
- "What is a KRI?" definition
- Categories of risk (with liquidity as one subsection)
- 1–2 paragraphs on liquidity ratios (usually LCR + LDR)
- Generic "how to choose KRIs" framework
- A closing pitch for the vendor's GRC platform

### Content gaps to exploit
1. **No piece deeply specializes in liquidity KRIs.** Every top-10 result treats liquidity as one chapter inside a broader KRI article.
2. **Formulas + worked numerical examples are missing.** Articles describe LCR and NSFR conceptually but rarely show the math with realistic numbers.
3. **Threshold tables (Low / Moderate / High) live in regulatory PDFs.** Surfacing these in an accessible blog format is uncontested.
4. **Bank vs. non-bank corporate split is poorly served.** Most articles are bank-only; corporate treasurers searching for KRIs are underserved.
5. **No top-ranking article uses the SVB 2023 collapse** as a case study — a vivid, current illustration of liquidity-KRI failure.
6. **Monitoring cadence guidance** (which KRIs to watch daily vs. weekly vs. monthly) is rarely framed as a practical matrix.
7. **Early-warning vs. lagging KRI distinction** is academic in the literature but rarely operationalized in blog content.

### Differentiation strategy
- Lead with a numbered list of 12–15 specific liquidity KRIs (snippet bait).
- Group KRIs into three audience-aware buckets: **regulated banks/FIs**, **corporates/non-banks**, and **early-warning behavioral indicators**.
- Include a formula + worked example for every KRI.
- Provide a single comparative table with KRI / formula / typical threshold / suggested cadence.
- Anchor the piece with the SVB 2023 case study and one corporate counterpart.
- Close with an implementation playbook (how to wire these into a risk register / board dashboard).

## 4. Supporting Elements

### Statistics & data points to include
1. Basel III sets LCR minimum at **100%** for internationally active banks (BIS, 2013).
2. Well-managed banks typically maintain LCR between **110–140%** (industry consensus, BBVA / OCC commentary).
3. NSFR became binding at **100%** under US final rule (OCC Bulletin 2021-9, effective 1 July 2021).
4. SVB lost roughly **$42 billion in deposits in a single day** on 9 March 2023 — a textbook deposit run-off KRI breach.
5. A common corporate-treasury rule of thumb: hold cash equal to **3–6 months of operating expenses** (multiple treasury sources).
6. LCR cash inflows are capped at **75%** of expected outflows (Basel III text).
7. ESRB systemic-liquidity framework (Feb 2025) lists deposit concentration, funding gap, and inverse NSFR as leading early-warning indicators that signal stress up to **one year in advance**.

## 7. KRI Reference Data (for the writer)

### Bank / FI KRIs

| KRI | Formula | Typical threshold | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) | HQLA / Net cash outflows over 30 days | ≥ 100% (regulatory); 110–140% comfort | Daily |
| Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) | Available stable funding / Required stable funding | ≥ 100% | Monthly |
| Loan-to-Deposit Ratio (LDR) | Total loans / Total deposits | 80–90% comfort; >100% stress | Weekly |
| Short-term wholesale funding ratio | Wholesale funding maturing < 1 yr / Total funding | < 25% comfort | Weekly |
| Core deposits / total deposits | Core deposits / Total deposits | > 70% comfort | Monthly |
| Top-10 depositor concentration | Top-10 deposit balances / Total deposits | < 20% comfort | Monthly |
| Encumbered assets ratio | Encumbered assets / Total assets | < 20% comfort | Monthly |

### Corporate / non-bank KRIs

| KRI | Formula | Typical threshold | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Days cash on hand | (Cash + marketable securities) / (Annual operating expenses ÷ 365) | 90–180 days | Weekly |
| Current ratio | Current assets / Current liabilities | > 1.5 comfort | Monthly |
| Quick ratio | (Cash + ST investments + AR) / Current liabilities | > 1.0 comfort | Monthly |
| Cash conversion cycle | DSO + DIO − DPO | Industry-relative | Monthly |
| Undrawn committed facility headroom | Undrawn committed facilities / Avg monthly cash burn | > 6× burn | Weekly |
| 13-week cash forecast variance | (Forecast − actual) / Forecast | < ±5% | Weekly |

### Behavioural / early-warning

| KRI | Signal |
|---|---|
| Deposit run-off rate | > 5% week-on-week deposit decline |
| Intraday liquidity peak usage | Sustained > 80% of available intraday limits |
| CDS spread widening | > 50 bps over 5-day MA |
| Wholesale funding spread | > 25 bps above peer median |
| Credit facility drawdown rate | Unscheduled drawdowns by counterparties |

## 8. Hook Development

### Opening hook options
1. **SVB-led**: "When Silicon Valley Bank collapsed on 10 March 2023, its liquidity coverage ratio still looked fine. The KRIs that mattered — deposit concentration, unrealized HTM losses, intraday outflow velocity — were flashing red weeks earlier."
2. **Statistic-led**: "94% of bank failures since 2008 had liquidity stress as a contributing cause, but most risk registers track only one or two liquidity KRIs."
3. **Question-led**: "If your board asked tomorrow which five numbers tell you whether the firm can pay its bills next month, could you answer?"

**Recommended**: Option 1 — SVB is timely, vivid, and immediately establishes that the standard regulatory KRI (LCR) alone is insufficient.

### Value proposition
A complete, formula-level catalogue of liquidity risk KRIs that a risk team can lift directly into a risk register or board dashboard — split clearly between regulated FIs, corporate treasuries, and early-warning behavioural indicators.

### Contrarian element
The mainstream view is "track LCR and NSFR." The contrarian wedge: **the KRIs that detect stress earliest are behavioural and concentration metrics, not the regulatory ratios.**
