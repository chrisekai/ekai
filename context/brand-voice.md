# ekai Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the ekai brand voice, tone, and messaging framework. Reference this when writing all content to ensure consistency.

The voice is modelled on Harvard Business Review Analytic Services and Gartner research notes: research-led, executive-grade, evidence-first, and explicitly tied to business consequence. Every assertion should be defensible with a number, a named expert, a regulator, or a framework clause.

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Research-led
- **What it means**: Every meaningful claim is grounded in data, a regulator, or a named expert. We do not editorialise without evidence.
- **How it sounds**: Specific. Quantified. Sourced. We open with a statistic or a finding, not an opinion.
- **Example**: "In a survey of 236 risk leaders conducted in March 2026, 56% reported at least one liquidity-stress event in the prior 12 months. Most never made it to the board pack."
- **Avoid**: Vague claims ("studies show", "many organizations", "experts believe"), generic platitudes, marketing superlatives.

### 2. Executive-grade
- **What it means**: We write for the people who own outcomes. CROs, CFOs, Heads of BCM, internal auditors, board risk committee members, regulators. Their time is constrained and their decisions are consequential.
- **How it sounds**: Direct. Structured. No throat-clearing. The first sentence answers the question; the rest provides the evidence.
- **Example**: "The cost of a poorly governed third-party engagement is not the contract value. It is the regulatory exposure, the customer churn, and the operational distraction. We have measured all three."
- **Avoid**: Junior-analyst tone, breathless explainer style, padding to look comprehensive, FAQ-as-content disguised as an article.

### 3. Business-consequence oriented
- **What it means**: Every concept is connected to a concrete outcome — revenue, capital, reputation, regulator, resilience. We do not treat risk as an academic taxonomy.
- **How it sounds**: External / internal impact frames. "When this fails, here is what breaks." Cause-and-effect chains that end in a balance-sheet line or a regulator's finding.
- **Example**: "When releases go wrong, the impact is both external and internal. Customers churn and the brand is damaged. Inside, engineering capacity bleeds into firefighting that should have been spent on the next product."
- **Avoid**: Process for its own sake, framework worship, pure compliance framing without commercial consequence.

### 4. Plain authority
- **What it means**: Complex topics in clear English. We respect the reader's intelligence by removing jargon, not by adding it. Frameworks (ISO 31000, COSO ERM, Basel III, ISO 22301) are referenced when relevant, never to perform expertise.
- **How it sounds**: Short sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences for nuance. Active voice. Concrete verbs. Frameworks cited by clause, not by namedrop.
- **Example**: "ISO 22301 §8.4 says you must define when, what, with whom, and how you will communicate. Most plans answer one of those four questions and skip the other three."
- **Avoid**: Consultancy buzzwords, four-syllable verbs where two-syllable ones work, hedged academic constructions, faux-British/American switching.

### 5. Decisive close
- **What it means**: Every piece earns its conclusion. We end with "the bottom line", "what this means", or a clearly named action — not with a polite restatement of the title.
- **How it sounds**: A single declarative sentence or short paragraph that names the implication and the next step.
- **Example**: "The bottom line: a risk function that cannot answer 'what changed since last week' in under five minutes is not a risk function. It is a reporting function pretending to be one."
- **Avoid**: "In conclusion" phrasing, decorative sign-offs, conclusions that require a re-read of the article to understand.

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine an experienced risk consultant briefing a board: precise, evidence-led, no padding, no jargon for jargon's sake. Says what matters and why it matters in business terms. Will pause for a clarifying number, will not pause for self-deprecation. Comfortable with both the framework page and the income statement.

### Tone Variations by Content Type

**How-to guides (templates, registers, KRI specs)**: instructive, sequenced, verifiable.
- "Define the scope before populating the rows. Confirm the rating scale. Calibrate to risk appetite."
- "Each control needs an owner and an evidence source. Without both, the control is decorative."
- "Test the channel before you need it. A channel you have not tested is a channel that will not work."

**Strategic / advisory content (board notes, executive briefings)**: authoritative, comparative, framework-grounded.
- "Three patterns separate firms with mature ERM from firms that are documenting it: appetite is quantified, controls are tested, and governance routes are timed."
- "The choice between concentrating with a strategic vendor and spreading risk across three is not a procurement question. It is a board-level resilience question."

**Industry analysis / regulatory updates**: analytical, contextual, leading-indicator-aware.
- "The SEC's first wave of cyber disclosure enforcement targeted disclosure controls, not the underlying incidents. The signal: how you talk about the breach matters as much as the breach itself."
- "OCC and FRB priorities for the 2026 examination cycle telegraph three pressure points: liquidity buffer formality, third-party concentration, and operational resilience. Boards should expect supervisory questions on all three before mid-year."

**Service / capability content (ekai's own offerings)**: outcome-focused, framework-anchored, not promotional.
- "Our ERM diagnostic produces a risk register, a KRI specification, and a 90-day remediation roadmap aligned to ISO 31000:2018."
- "Engagements close with a board memo that names the residual risks above appetite and the owner accountable for each."

## Messaging Framework

### Core Brand Messages

#### Message 1: Risk visibility is competitive advantage
- **Concept**: Strong risk management is not a regulatory tax. It is the operating system that lets a business take faster, larger, more confident decisions.
- **Key points**:
  - Firms that quantify risk appetite move faster on capital deployment than those that talk in adjectives.
  - Risk-mature firms have lower cost of capital, lower insurance premiums, and shorter regulator examinations.
  - The board that knows what is changing each week governs better than the board that gets a 60-page pack each quarter.
- **Usage**: Lead messaging for content aimed at CFOs, CEOs, and board members who view risk as cost.

#### Message 2: Frameworks must meet reality
- **Concept**: ISO 31000, COSO ERM, Basel III, and ISO 22301 are frameworks, not implementations. Translation is the work.
- **Key points**:
  - A framework citation in a policy that no operator can act on is a finding waiting to happen.
  - US supervisory expectations (SEC, OCC, FRB, FDIC, NCUA, FINRA, CFPB) sit on top of international standards, not below them.
  - Implementation is decided at the line item: who owns the control, what evidence proves it works, when does it get tested.
- **Usage**: How-to and template content, especially when standards are being referenced.

#### Message 3: The cost of doing nothing is concrete
- **Concept**: Every risk choice has a quantifiable downside. We surface it.
- **Key points**:
  - Liquidity, credit, operational, cyber, and conduct events have observable historical losses; we benchmark against them.
  - Regulatory exposure is now measurable in fines, capital add-ons, and management bandwidth.
  - The "soft" costs (reputation, churn, talent) are increasingly priced in by analysts and rating agencies.
- **Usage**: Persuasive content, executive briefings, board memos, sales-supporting analyses.

#### Message 4: Boards govern what they can see
- **Concept**: Risk reporting is the interface between the risk function and the board. Most interfaces are broken.
- **Key points**:
  - A board pack that reports lagging indicators only is reporting yesterday.
  - Heatmaps without thresholds are art. Heatmaps with thresholds are a control.
  - Five well-chosen KRIs beat fifty poorly defined ones.
- **Usage**: KRI design content, board reporting redesigns, dashboard engagements.

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: mix short declarative sentences ("Risk owners own risks. Risk functions oversee them.") with longer ones that explain mechanism.
- **Active voice preferred**: "The Board approved the appetite" not "The appetite was approved by the Board."
- **Average length**: 17 to 22 words per sentence; max 28 except in deliberately rhythmic stretches.
- **Clarity first**: if a sentence requires re-reading, split it.

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2 to 4 sentences typically. Five at most. No walls of text.
- **One idea per paragraph**: each paragraph carries one claim and the evidence for it.
- **Transitions**: prefer "but", "yet", "and", "still" over "however", "moreover", "furthermore" where the rhythm allows it.
- **White space**: short paragraphs aid both human scanning and AI parsing.

### Word Choice
- **Specific over general**: "47% of NBFIs surveyed" not "many institutions"; "below 105% LCR" not "low liquidity".
- **Active verbs**: report, breach, escalate, calibrate, verify, govern, name. Not utilize, leverage, operationalize.
- **Concrete consequences**: name the regulator, the line item, the loss event. Avoid "negative impact" when "regulatory fine", "customer churn", or "capital add-on" is what you mean.
- **Avoid fluff**: cut "very", "really", "actually", "essentially", "literally". Most of them are tells.

### Terminology

**Say This → Not That**

- risk appetite (quantified) → risk tolerance (when used loosely as a synonym)
- key risk indicator (KRI) → risk metric (when threshold and cadence are defined)
- residual risk → net risk
- inherent risk → gross risk
- three lines of defense → three lines model (acceptable; some clients prefer the older form)
- risk owner (first-line, accountable executive) → risk department (the second-line oversight function)
- enterprise risk management (ERM) → risk management (when scope is enterprise-wide)
- business continuity management system (BCMS) → business continuity (when ISO 22301 conformance matters)

## Content Formatting

### Headlines
- **Be specific**: "15 Liquidity Risk KRI Examples (with Formulas)" beats "Liquidity Risk Indicators".
- **Lead with the deliverable**: "Crisis Communication Plan Template", "Risk Register: 12 Worked Examples".
- **Year for time-sensitive content**: include the year on regulatory or framework-update pieces ("…2026", "…post-CIRCIA").
- **Keep concise**: aim for under 60 characters for SEO; under 70 for headers on pillar pieces.

### Subheadings
- **Question or concrete claim**: "What is a KRI?" or "Three lines of defense, in practice".
- **One section, one idea**: AI parsers and skim readers both reward this.
- **Keyword in 2 to 3 H2s** of any SEO piece, integrated naturally.

### Lists
- **Use bullets when sequence does not matter**; numbered lists when it does.
- **Capitalise the first word**; finish with a period if the bullet is a complete sentence.
- **Parallel structure**: all sentences or all fragments, never mixed.
- **Bullets must do work**: do not bullet for visual decoration. If three bullets all say the same thing in slightly different words, write a sentence.

### Calls-to-Action
- **Name the next artifact**: "Run /risk-register liquidity risk", not "explore our services".
- **Specify the time cost**: "a 30-minute diagnostic call", not "let's chat".
- **Anchor in framework**: "ISO 31000-aligned register review", not "best-practice review".

## Voice Examples

### Excellent ekai voice ✅

> When SVB collapsed in March 2023, its Liquidity Coverage Ratio still looked compliant. The KRIs that mattered (deposit concentration, unrealised held-to-maturity losses, intraday outflow velocity) were flashing red weeks earlier. None made the board pack.
>
> The lesson is not that LCR is a bad metric. It is that a single regulator-mandated ratio cannot describe a multi-dimensional risk. Boards that govern liquidity through one number are governing the audit, not the business.
>
> The bottom line: pair every regulatory ratio with at least one early-warning indicator that your treasury team trusts and your board can interpret. If you cannot name yours in under 30 seconds, you are flying lagging-only.

**Why this works**:
- Opens with a named, dated event with consequence.
- Specific KRIs are listed, not gestured at.
- Frames the lesson as a governance issue, not a technical one.
- "The bottom line" delivers a decisive, action-oriented close.
- Sentences vary; no jargon needed to make the point.

### Not ekai voice ❌

> In today's complex regulatory environment, organizations must leverage best-in-class risk management practices to ensure compliance and drive value. Our holistic approach helps stakeholders navigate uncertainty by aligning frameworks with strategic objectives, ultimately empowering data-driven decision-making across the enterprise.
>
> By partnering with seasoned advisors, leaders can unlock unprecedented visibility into their risk landscape and transform compliance from a cost center into a strategic differentiator.

**Why this fails**:
- Zero specifics: no number, no regulator, no framework cited.
- Buzzword density: "leverage", "best-in-class", "holistic", "unlock", "unprecedented".
- Passive abstraction: "stakeholders navigate uncertainty" tells the reader nothing.
- No claim that could be true or false; therefore no claim at all.
- Closing line is a slogan, not an action.

## Audience Understanding

### Who We Write For

**Primary audience**: Senior risk, finance, audit, and resilience leaders at US-regulated financial institutions and large corporations.
- Chief Risk Officers, Heads of Risk, Heads of BCM, Heads of Internal Audit.
- CFOs and Treasurers at US banks, BHCs, insurers, broker-dealers, credit unions, and Fortune 1000 corporates.
- Board members and audit committee members of public and large private companies.
- Operating under SEC, OCC, FRB, FDIC, NCUA, FINRA, CFPB supervision and ISO 31000, ISO 22301, COSO ERM, NIST CSF, Basel III where relevant.

**Secondary audiences**:
- **Operational risk and compliance officers**: practitioners building registers, KRIs, and policies.
- **Regulators and supervisors**: who consult published thought leadership when scoping examinations.
- **Consultants and advisors**: peers who reference ekai content in client work.
- **Procurement and vendor risk teams**: especially those responsible for outsourcing governance.

### What They Care About

**Top priorities**:
1. Defensible risk artifacts that survive board, regulator, and external-audit scrutiny.
2. Frameworks (ISO 31000, COSO ERM, Basel III, ISO 22301) translated into practical, line-item actions.
3. KRIs that move before the loss event, not after.
4. Reporting that gives the board control, not just visibility.
5. Sound third-party and outsourcing governance.

**Pain points**:
1. Risk registers full of categories disguised as risks; ratings clustered in the center of the heatmap.
2. KRIs that tell them what already happened.
3. Policies that read well but no one can operate.
4. Regulators asking for evidence the firm cannot produce on demand.
5. Boards that receive sixty pages and govern none of them.

### How to Serve Them

- **Respect their time**: lead with the answer, follow with the evidence.
- **Make it actionable**: every concept should land in a register row, a KRI threshold, a policy clause, or a board metric.
- **Cite the source**: if a clause or threshold is regulatory, name the regulator and the section.
- **Acknowledge constraints**: budget, headcount, legacy systems, regulator transitions. Do not write as if these do not exist.
- **Speak with authority, not arrogance**: we are practitioners writing to practitioners.

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

- [ ] **Voice**: research-led, executive-grade, business-consequence-oriented, plain-authoritative, decisive at the close.
- [ ] **Tone**: matches the content type and the seniority of the audience.
- [ ] **Evidence**: every meaningful claim is grounded in a number, named source, framework clause, or regulator citation.
- [ ] **Clarity**: a senior risk leader can read it once and act on it.
- [ ] **Accuracy**: framework references correct (ISO 31000:2018, COSO ERM 2017, BCBS 144 / 238, ISO 22301:2019, NIST CSF 2.0). Regulator citations correct (SEC, OCC, FRB, FDIC, NCUA, FINRA, CFPB).
- [ ] **Examples**: at least one named real-world case (SVB 2023, Maersk 2017 NotPetya, R.R. Donnelley SEC settlement, etc.) or one fully-worked numerical example.
- [ ] **Action**: the reader knows what to do next, with whom, by when.
- [ ] **Messaging**: aligns with one or more of the four core brand messages.
- [ ] **Terminology**: uses the preferred forms in the Say This → Not That table.
- [ ] **Empowerment**: the reader leaves more capable, not more impressed by the author.
